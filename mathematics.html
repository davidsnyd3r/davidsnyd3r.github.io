Monotone Convergence Theorem

We will see the power of the Monotone Convergence Theorem to obtain the Golden Ratio as a limit of nested radicals.

Define 
\[ x := \sqrt{1+\sqrt{1+\sqrt{1+\sqrt{1+\dots}}}} \]

Define the sequence:
\begin{align*}
a_0 &= 1 \\
a_1 &= \sqrt{1} = 1 \\
a_2 &= \sqrt{1+\sqrt{1}} = \sqrt{2} \\
a_3 &= \sqrt{1+\sqrt{1+\sqrt{1}}} = \sqrt{1+\sqrt{2}} \\
a_4 &= \sqrt{1+\sqrt{1+\sqrt{1+\sqrt{1}}}} = \sqrt{1+\sqrt{1+\sqrt{2}}} \\
&\dots
\end{align*}
The pattern continues and satisfies the recurrence relation:
\[ a_n = \sqrt{1+a_{n-1}}, \quad \forall n \geq 1 \]

This begs the question, does \( x = \lim_{n\to\infty}a_n \) exist? If so, what is a closed form of \( x \)?

\begin{proof}
We show that \( a_n \) is increasing monotonically. 

Let's proceed by induction.

Base Case: \( n = 1 \)
\[ a_1 < a_2 \]

Induction step: 
Assume the induction hypothesis holds for \( n \leq k-1 \). 
\[ a_{k+1} = \sqrt{1+ a_k}, \quad a_{k} = \sqrt{1+a_{k-1}} \]

Since the square root function is monotonically increasing and \( a_{k-1} < a_k \) by the induction hypothesis, it follows that \( a_n \) is a monotonically increasing sequence. We conclude the sequence is monotonically increasing for all \( n \in \mathbb{N} \) by the Principle of Mathematical Induction.

The sequence is increasing monotonically, but how do we know it does not trail off to infinity? We claim the sequence is bounded by \( 2 \).

We prove this by mathematical induction.

Base case: \( n=1 \)
\[ a_1 = 1 < 2 \]

Induction Step:

Assume the induction hypothesis holds for \( n \leq k-1 \).
\[ a_{k} = \sqrt{1 + a_{k-1}} \leq \sqrt{1+2} = \sqrt{3} < \sqrt{4} = 2 \]

The induction hypothesis holds, and we conclude that \( a_n \) is bounded for all \( n \in \mathbb{N} \).

By the Monotonic Convergence Theorem, the limit exists:
\[ x = \lim_{n\to\infty} a_{n+1} = \lim_{n\to\infty} \sqrt{1+a_{n}} = \sqrt{\lim_{n\to\infty} (1 + a_n)} = \sqrt{1 + \lim_{n\to\infty} a_n } = \sqrt{1 + x} \]

The square root function is continuous, justifying the passing of the limit inside. This yields:
\[ x^2 - x - 1 = 0 \]

Solving gives two roots, \( x_1 = \frac{1-\sqrt{5}}{2} \) and \( x_2 = \frac{1 + \sqrt{5}}{2} \). Since \( a_n \) is strictly positive, it follows that \( x = \frac{1 + \sqrt{5}}{2} \). This limit is known as the "Golden Ratio".
\end{proof}
