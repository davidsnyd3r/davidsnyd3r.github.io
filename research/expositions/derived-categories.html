A Gentle Introduction to Derived Categories

Derived categories are a central tool in modern algebraic geometry and representation theory. They allow us to treat complexes of sheaves (or modules) as primary objects, rather than just the sheaves themselves.

Why do we need them?

In homological algebra, we often study short exact sequences:

0→A→B→C→00 \to A \to B \to C \to 00→A→B→C→0

However, many functors we care about (like tensor product ⊗\otimes⊗ or Hom) are not exact—they are only right or left exact. Derived categories provide the correct framework to fix this defect using derived functors (RHomR\text{Hom}RHom, ⊗L\mathop{\otimes}\limits^L⊗L​).

The Definition

Formally, the derived category D(A)D(\mathcal{A})D(A) of an abelian category A\mathcal{A}A is obtained from the category of chain complexes Ch(A)Ch(\mathcal{A})Ch(A) by inverting quasi-isomorphisms.

D(A)=Ch(A)[Q−1]D(\mathcal{A}) = Ch(\mathcal{A})[Q^{-1}]D(A)=Ch(A)[Q−1]

This localization process is analogous to constructing rationals Q\mathbb{Q}Q from integers Z\mathbb{Z}Z.

Applications to Quiver Representations

In the context of Quiver Representations, derived categories allow us to study the bounded derived category of representations of a quiver QQQ.

Db(Repk(Q))D^b(\text{Rep}_k(Q))Db(Repk​(Q))

This is crucial for understanding tilting theory and cluster algebras.

Connection to TDA

Recent work suggests that the decomposition of persistence modules in Topological Data Analysis can be viewed through the lens of derived categories of graded modules.
